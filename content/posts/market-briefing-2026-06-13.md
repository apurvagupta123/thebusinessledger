## A Rocket Company Worth More Than Most Countries Just Started Trading on the Stock Market

The markets are closed today — it's Saturday — but what happened on Friday was too big to wait until Monday.

SpaceX, Elon Musk's rocket company, went public on Friday in the biggest stock market debut in history. The company sold shares to the public, raised $75 billion in a single day, and the stock shot up 19% before the closing bell.

That's not a typo. Seventy-five billion dollars. In one day.

But here's the thing: the week ahead brings news that could affect your rent, your grocery bill, and the interest on your credit card far more than a rocket company's stock price ever will.

---

### Last Week's Markets — What Happened and Why

Think of the stock market like a giant scoreboard. When the score goes up, it means investors — people who own pieces of companies — are feeling good about the future. When it goes down, they're worried.

Here's how Friday closed out:

| Index | Approx. Level | Change Friday | What it means |
|-------|-------|--------|---------------|
| Dow Jones (30 major US companies) | ~51,200 | **+0.7%** | Investors ended the week feeling hopeful |

| NASDAQ (Mostly tech companies) | ~N/A | **+0.3%** | Tech held steady despite SpaceX pulling money away |

Two things drove the good mood this week: the SpaceX debut, and growing hope that the US and Iran might finally stop fighting.

### The Big Story: SpaceX Goes Public

Here's what "going public" means: a private company (one you couldn't buy shares of before) decides to sell pieces of itself to regular investors on the stock market. That process is called an IPO — an "Initial Public Offering," which is just a fancy way of saying "the first time you can buy a slice of this company."

SpaceX set its price at **$135 per share**. When trading began Friday morning, the stock opened at $150 and quickly surged over 30% before settling to close at **$161.11** — up **19% on day one**, under the ticker symbol **SPCX** on the Nasdaq.

The company is now valued at roughly **$1.77 trillion**. To put that in perspective, that's more than the entire economy of Australia.

**Who benefits?** Early investors, employees who held SpaceX shares for years, and Elon Musk, whose net worth reportedly crossed the $1 trillion mark for the first time. It's a milestone that no human being has ever achieved before.

**Who gets squeezed?** Shares of Nvidia, Apple, Microsoft, and Broadcom all dipped on Friday. When a massive new company starts trading, investors sometimes sell their existing stocks to buy the new one — think of it like everyone rushing to try a new restaurant, leaving older favourites slightly quieter for a day.

**For ordinary people?** The SpaceX IPO itself won't change your grocery bill or rent tomorrow. But it signals something bigger: the era of "space economy" stocks has arrived. Companies building satellites, rocket services, and space logistics are about to become a regular part of pension funds and retirement accounts — which means your future savings might one day be partly tied to how well humanity does beyond Earth.

### The Iran Situation — And Why It Affects What You Pay for Petrol

The world's oil supply passes through a narrow stretch of water called the **Strait of Hormuz** — a channel between Iran and the Arabian Peninsula. About **10 to 11 million barrels of oil pass through there every day**, or roughly 20% of the world's oil trade.

Since the US and Iran began their conflict, Iran has kept that strait partially blocked. That's like someone putting a thumb over a garden hose — less oil gets through, prices rise, and everyone who needs to drive, fly, or heat their home pays more.

Right now, **Brent crude oil** (the international oil benchmark) sits at around **$97.94 a barrel**. That's still about one-third higher than it was before this conflict began, though it's fallen from its peak as peace talks have progressed.

The good news: US and Iranian officials have been talking. There are reports of a possible truce memorandum. The bad news: the US also imposed fresh sanctions on Iran's oil arm as recently as June 1 — mixed signals that have kept oil traders nervous.

**What this means for you, today:**
- **Petrol prices** remain elevated because oil is expensive. A deal would bring relief.
- **Food prices** stay high, too. Food is grown on farms, transported by lorries, shipped on vessels — all of which run on oil. When oil is expensive, everything that moves gets pricier.
- **Airline tickets** remain costly. A confirmed peace deal could shave pounds off the cost of your next holiday flight.
- **Factory workers and delivery drivers** in countries that import Middle Eastern oil — including India, Japan, South Korea, and much of Europe — are paying the price through higher living costs.

**If this continues for years:** Countries that rely heavily on Middle Eastern oil will keep running trade deficits (spending more money abroad than they earn). That weakens their currencies, drives up import costs, and puts pressure on governments to cut social spending. Children growing up in oil-importing nations today may face a generation of higher energy costs unless either the conflict resolves or renewable energy scales up fast enough to fill the gap.

### Inflation at 4.2% — What That Number Actually Means

The US government reported on June 10 that **inflation is running at 4.2% per year**. Here's what that means in plain English:

If a bag of groceries cost you $100 a year ago, it costs you about **$104.20 today**.

The Federal Reserve (America's central bank — think of it as the bank that controls how expensive it is to borrow money) has a target of **2% inflation**. They're nowhere near it. At 4.2%, everyday things are still getting more expensive faster than most people's wages are rising.

### The Week Ahead: This Is the One to Watch

Here's what's happening next week that could genuinely affect your finances:

**Monday, June 16** — US manufacturing data is released. This tells us whether American factories are busy or slowing down. If they're slowing, that could mean job cuts ahead.

**Tuesday–Wednesday, June 16–17** — The **Federal Reserve meets**. This is the big one.

The Fed's job is to keep inflation low and employment high. With inflation at 4.2% — double their target — they are under enormous pressure. The meeting is led by Kevin Warsh, the new Fed chair.

**What might they do?** Most analysts expect them to keep interest rates where they are, but the language they use will be carefully parsed by markets. There's growing talk — for the first time in years — of a **rate hike** (raising interest rates). If that happens:

- **Your mortgage** gets more expensive if it has a variable rate
- **Car loans and personal loans** become costlier to take out
- **Credit card debt** gets more expensive to carry
- **Savings accounts** might pay slightly better interest

The Fed is essentially trying to slow down how fast prices rise by making it more expensive to borrow money. It's a blunt instrument — and ordinary families feel it first.

**Wednesday–Thursday** — Major tech companies report their quarterly earnings: **Tesla, Microsoft, Meta, and Apple**. These four companies are so large that their results can move the entire market up or down on their own. If they report strong profits, markets will likely rise. If they disappoint, expect a rough ride.

### What to Watch This Week — And Why It Matters to You

**Watch the Fed statement on Wednesday.** If they hint at raising rates, expect mortgage rates, car loan rates, and credit card rates to climb in the weeks that follow. This directly affects millions of families.

**Watch the Iran headlines.** Any sign of a real peace agreement would send oil prices lower almost immediately. That means cheaper petrol, cheaper food transport, and lower airline fares within weeks. Conversely, a breakdown in talks would push prices the other way.

**Watch Apple earnings on Thursday.** Apple sells more iPhones and devices than almost any company on Earth. If their sales are weakening, it could signal that consumers are tightening their belts — a warning sign that the economy is cooling.

The week ahead is unusually consequential. The decisions made in a meeting room in Washington on Wednesday will echo in kitchen tables and fuel pumps across the country for months. Come back Monday and we'll walk you through it, step by step.
