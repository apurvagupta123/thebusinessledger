## Oracle's Record Quarter Isn't Enough — Wall Street Eyes the Bill

Oracle beat on every metric that matters. Revenue hit a record $19.2 billion, cloud infrastructure surged 93%, and AI contract backlog topped $638 billion. Yet investors punished the stock more than 7% in after-hours trading Tuesday — and the overhang dragged into Thursday's session. The reason: CapEx ballooned to $55.7 billion for the fiscal year, blowing past the company's own $50 billion guidance, and management plans to raise another $50 billion in debt and equity to fund its AI data center buildout. In a week already rattled by hot inflation and Middle East tensions, it was one bill too many.

### Key Movers

**Oracle (ORCL)** dropped more than 7% in after-hours trading Wednesday and continued to weigh on the Nasdaq Thursday. Despite record Q4 revenue of $19.2 billion (+21% YoY) and non-GAAP EPS of $2.11 (beating the $1.96 consensus), investors fixated on a capital expenditure figure of $55.7 billion for fiscal 2026 — well above the company's prior $50 billion guidance — and a plan to raise $50 billion through new debt and equity financing. The stock was trading near $191.49 in early Thursday activity.

**Adobe (ADBE)** was in focus heading into its Q2 fiscal 2026 earnings release after the close. Street consensus called for EPS of $5.81 on revenue of $6.45 billion. The stock has shed roughly 30% year-to-date as investors reprice creative software for AI disruption risk — making this the most watched after-market print of the week.

**Chip stocks** continued their tentative recovery. The PHLX Semiconductor Index (SOX) bounced early in the week after its 10% Friday plunge, though the recovery has been uneven. Marvell (MRVL) and Micron (MU) led early gains.

### What Moved Markets

Wednesday's 1.6%–2.0% selloff across all three major indices was a convergence of three forces: a hot CPI print, renewed U.S.-Iran military tension, and Oracle's sticker-shock capital spending disclosure.

The inflation number told a nuanced story. The 4.2% headline — a three-year high — was largely energy-driven, a direct spillover from the prolonged U.S.-Iran conflict that has kept WTI crude above $90. Core CPI, however, came in at +0.2% MoM, below both estimates and April's pace, which provided some comfort. The NASDAQ 100, notably, reversed early losses to close near positive territory as investors parsed the core number as "less bad than feared."

But the bond market didn't budge. The 10-year Treasury yield held at 4.53%, keeping pressure on rate-sensitive growth stocks and reinforcing the Fed's dilemma. With three months of strong jobs data and inflation running at double the Fed's 2% target, rate-hike odds have snapped from near-zero to 72% in a matter of weeks.

Thursday opened with a shot of relief. U.S. officials confirmed the initial phase of strikes against Iran had concluded, lifting S&P 500 futures. A cooler-than-expected PPI print added to early momentum. But the Oracle hangover — and the broader question of what $55 billion in AI CapEx spending means for the sector's return on investment — kept bulls from pressing their advantage. The earnings from Adobe and Lennar after Thursday's close would set the tone for Friday's open.

The week ahead of next week's Federal Reserve meeting is now fully defined: a Fed that was debating cuts as recently as May is now managing market expectations around a potential hike.
