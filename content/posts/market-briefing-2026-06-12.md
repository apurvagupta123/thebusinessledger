## History Made: SpaceX Goes Public as Trump Calls End to the Iran War

Friday, June 12 delivered two seismic events simultaneously: the largest IPO in market history and the most consequential geopolitical development of 2026. Elon Musk's SpaceX began trading on the Nasdaq under ticker SPCX at its fixed offering price of $135 per share — raising $75 billion and valuing the company at roughly $1.75 trillion — while President Trump declared the United States had "ended the war with Iran," sending oil prices sharply lower and equity futures surging before the opening bell.

The convergence of those two catalysts made June 12 one of the most closely watched trading days in years.

### SpaceX IPO: The Biggest Debut in History

SpaceX priced at $135 per share Wednesday night, selling 555.6 million Class A shares and raising approximately $75 billion — surpassing every prior IPO on record. At that price, the company carries a post-money valuation of $1.75 trillion, which would make it the seventh-largest U.S. company by market cap and place it above Tesla's current $1.6 trillion valuation.

Demand was extraordinary. The offering was reported as 3.5 to four times oversubscribed, with retail investors alone submitting more than $100 billion in orders. SpaceX has a deliberately small public float of approximately 4%, meaning MSCI index inclusion — expected to begin Saturday, June 13 — will create structural buying demand from passive funds in the coming days.

Friday marked the company's first live trading session on Nasdaq. The combination of one of the most anticipated IPOs in technology history and the Iran peace news gave the debut an unusual dual tailwind.

### Iran Deal: Markets Price the End of a Conflict

Throughout the week, markets had been whipsawed by U.S.-Iran developments. After the Dow fell nearly 900 points on June 10 as the U.S. signaled further strikes, Thursday brought a dramatic reversal when Trump called off planned strikes and said a deal was "essentially agreed." By Friday morning, Trump went further, stating flatly that the U.S. had "ended the war with Iran today."

The market reaction was swift and decisive. Oil prices, which had surged past $92 per barrel (Brent) as the Strait of Hormuz faced disruption, moved sharply lower on the peace signal. Energy and defense stocks gave back ground, while transport, airlines, and consumer sectors — hammered by months of elevated fuel costs — rallied on the prospect of relief at the pump.

The conflict had been the primary inflationary force in 2026, pushing gasoline up more than a dollar a gallon and driving May CPI to 4.2% year-over-year — the highest reading since April 2023. A credible deal ending the conflict would materially alter the Fed's calculus ahead of next week's FOMC meeting.

### Key Movers

**Oracle (ORCL)** — The week's biggest loser among large caps. Shares fell nearly 12% on June 11 after the company beat Q4 estimates but announced plans to raise nearly $40 billion in 2027 to fund AI infrastructure expansion, including an at-the-market equity offering. The surprise capital raise rattled investors already cautious about AI spending cycles. Oracle closed near $184 premarket.

**Adobe (ADBE)** — Delivered a strong Q2 beat after Thursday's close: non-GAAP EPS of $5.96 vs. the $5.83 consensus estimate, revenue of $6.62 billion (+11% YoY) against a $6.46 billion estimate. AI-first annual recurring revenue more than tripled year-over-year, topping $500 million. However, the unexpected departure of Adobe's CFO overshadowed the strong numbers and sent shares materially lower in after-hours and Friday premarket trading.

**Intel (INTC), Micron (MU), Lam Research (LRCX)** — Semiconductor names rebounded sharply Thursday. Intel gained 10.3%, Micron surged 11%, and Lam Research added 12.7%, partially reversing the prior week's devastating semiconductor selloff that wiped $1 trillion in market cap on June 5.

### Economic Pulse

**May PPI (Released June 11):** Producer prices rose 1.1% month-over-month, well above the 0.7% consensus, putting the 12-month wholesale inflation rate at 6.5% — the highest since November 2022. Energy costs linked to the Iran conflict drove the headline surge, though core PPI (ex-food and energy) rose a more contained 0.4%.

**May CPI (Released June 11):** Consumer prices rose 4.2% year-over-year — highest since April 2023 — driven largely by a 27% surge in airfares and elevated gasoline prices. Real wages declined as wages grew only 3.4% year-over-year.

**ECB Rate Hike (June 11):** The European Central Bank became one of the first major central banks to respond to Iran-driven inflation, raising its benchmark rate to 2.25% from 2.00%. The Fed, Bank of Japan, and Bank of England meet next week.

**University of Michigan Consumer Sentiment (June 12 Preliminary):** Markets expected a modest bounce to approximately 46.0 from May's record low of 44.8. A sustained reading below 50 reflects historically severe consumer distress. Cost-of-living concerns remain the dominant driver, with 57% of consumers citing high prices as a first-order problem.

### What Moved Markets

The week of June 9–12 told a story of extreme volatility driven by one variable: the U.S.-Iran conflict. The Nasdaq lost 4% on June 5 in its worst single-day session since April 2025. The Dow shed 900 points on June 10. Then sentiment reversed violently when Trump signaled a peace deal Thursday, adding 930 Dow points in a single session, before declaring the war effectively over Friday morning.

The conflict has been the defining macro story of 2026 — disrupting Strait of Hormuz shipping, driving energy inflation to multi-year highs, forcing central banks globally into reactive rate hikes, and depressing consumer sentiment to near-record lows. A credible, lasting deal would represent the most significant positive macro catalyst since early spring.

Friday's session piled on a once-in-a-generation corporate event: the SpaceX IPO. With MSCI inclusion beginning Saturday and roughly $250 billion in demand chasing $75 billion of supply, the structural mechanics favored a strong debut regardless of broader market conditions.

The combination — peace optimism plus historic capital markets event — gave equity markets unusual bilateral support going into the weekend.
